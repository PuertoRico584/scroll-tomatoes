The Poop Problem

The average pig on a hog farm produces 10 times the waste of a human. That's a lot of poop. But where does all of this poop go? In most cases, it ends up in the town's water systems, causing unsafe drinking water, not to mention the awful smell. At Butler Farms, farmer, Tom Butler, has created a clever system to put this poop to good use.

Let's take a look into the process

From poop to profit â€” Butler Farms has found a way to turn methane gas produce from hog waste into CO2. This CO2 is used to power the whole farm and even a little extra.

Covering the Lagoon

Butler Farms collects the methane by pushing all of the hog waste into a lagoon, a common practice at hog farms. What Butler Farms does differently is they cover their lagoons so that the methane produced from the waste is trapped in a sort of gross bubble. Not only do the lagoon covers help trap the methane, but reduce 85 percent of the odor and reduce the overall amount of waste by excluding rainfall, which just adds to the smelly water.

180 KW Gen

A 180 KW gen-set, or large generator, collects the methane pumped into it and converts it into carbon dioxide through combustion, which powers the generator. This energy is used to power the farm and even creates a little extra to be sold off.

Poop to Profit

The CO2-turned-power is used throughout the farm, such as machinery and the Butler house. Butler Farms has a Power Purchase Agreement with South River Electric Membership Cooperative, meaning they turn poop into profit.
